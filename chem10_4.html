Energy Changes and Electrochemistry

Deep Understanding of Energy Changes and Electrochemistry

Energy Changes in Chemical Reactions

Energy changes refer to the difference in energy between reactants and products in a chemical reaction. Energy changes in reactions are essential in determining the spontaneity and feasibility of a reaction. These changes are typically manifested as heat changes (heat of reaction) or work done.

1. Types of Energy Changes

  • Exothermic Reactions: These reactions release energy (usually in the form of heat). The total energy of the products is less than that of the reactants. The heat of reaction (ΔH) is negative.
    • Example: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O + energy
    ΔH < 0
  • Endothermic Reactions: These reactions absorb energy from the surroundings. The total energy of the products is higher than the reactants. The heat of reaction (ΔH) is positive.
    • Example: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
    ΔH > 0

2. Heat of Reaction (ΔH)

The heat of reaction is the change in heat content (enthalpy) when a chemical reaction occurs at constant pressure. It is calculated by:

ΔH = H(products) - H(reactants)

If ΔH is negative, it indicates an exothermic reaction. If ΔH is positive, it indicates an endothermic reaction.

3. Hess's Law

Hess's Law states that the total enthalpy change for a reaction is the sum of the enthalpy changes for the individual steps of the reaction. This is based on the principle of conservation of energy.

ΔHtotal = ΣΔHsteps

4. Enthalpy Change in Bond Dissociation

The energy required to break one mole of a particular bond in the gas phase is known as the bond dissociation energy. The overall enthalpy change can be estimated by summing the bond dissociation energies of the bonds broken and formed.

ΔHreaction = ΣDbonds broken - ΣDbonds formed

Electrochemistry

1. Redox Reactions

Redox reactions are those in which there is a transfer of electrons between reactants. Oxidation refers to the loss of electrons, while reduction refers to the gain of electrons.

  • Oxidation Half-Reaction: 
    Oxidation: M → Mⁿ⁺ + ne⁻
  • Reduction Half-Reaction: 
    Reduction: Mⁿ⁺ + ne⁻ → M

2. Electrochemical Cells

  • Galvanic Cells (Voltaic Cells): These cells generate electrical energy from spontaneous redox reactions. They consist of two half-cells, each containing a metal electrode immersed in an electrolyte solution.
    • Example: Daniell Cell
    Zn(s) + Cu²⁺ (aq) → Zn²⁺ (aq) + Cu(s)
  • Electrolytic Cells: These cells require external electrical energy to drive a non-spontaneous reaction. Electrolysis involves the use of electrical energy to cause a chemical change, such as the electrolysis of water.
    • Example: Electrolysis of Water
    2H₂O(l) → 2H₂(g) + O₂(g)

3. Nernst Equation

The Nernst equation allows the calculation of the cell potential under non-standard conditions.

E = Eº - (0.0592 / n) log Q

Where:

  • E: Cell potential under non-standard conditions
  • Eº: Standard cell potential
  • n: Number of moles of electrons transferred
  • Q: Reaction quotient

4. Standard Electrode Potentials (Eº)

The standard electrode potential is the potential of a half-cell under standard conditions (1M concentration, 1 atm pressure, and 25°C). The standard electrode potentials help predict the direction of electron flow and which species will be reduced or oxidized in a reaction.

For a half-reaction: Mⁿ⁺ + ne⁻ → M(s) Eº = Standard reduction potential

5. Electrochemical Series

The electrochemical series ranks elements based on their standard electrode potentials. The species with the higher potential will undergo reduction, while the one with the lower potential will undergo oxidation.

Important Formulas in Energy Changes and Electrochemistry

  • Energy Change (Heat of Reaction): 
    ΔH = H(products) - H(reactants)
  • Nernst Equation: 
    E = Eº - (0.0592 / n) log Q
  • Cell Potential (Standard Cell Potential): 
    Eºcell = Eºcathode - Eºanode
  • Gibbs Free Energy and Cell Potential: 
    ΔG = -nFE
  • Faraday's Law of Electrolysis: 
    m = (M × I × t) / (n × F)
