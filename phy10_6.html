Electromagnetic Waves and Geometrical Optics

Electromagnetic Waves

Electromagnetic waves are waves of electric and magnetic fields that propagate through space. These waves can travel through a vacuum (such as light from the Sun reaching the Earth) and through various materials. They form part of the electromagnetic spectrum, which includes a wide range of wave types, from low-frequency radio waves to high-frequency gamma rays.

Key Properties:
  • Electric and Magnetic Fields: An electromagnetic wave consists of electric (E) and magnetic (B) fields oscillating perpendicular to each other and to the direction of wave propagation.
  • Wave Equation: The behavior of electromagnetic waves can be described by the wave equation:
    ∂²E/∂x² - (1/c²) ∂²E/∂t² = 0
    where E is the electric field, c is the speed of light in a vacuum, x is position, and t is time.
  • Speed of Light: The speed of electromagnetic waves in a vacuum is denoted as c, which is approximately:
    c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s
  • Frequency and Wavelength: The relationship between the frequency f, wavelength λ, and the speed of light is given by:
    c = λf
The Electromagnetic Spectrum:
  • Radio waves: f = 10⁴ Hz to 10¹² Hz
  • Microwaves
  • Infrared (IR)
  • Visible light
  • Ultraviolet (UV)
  • X-rays
  • Gamma rays

The energy of a photon in an electromagnetic wave is related to its frequency by:

E = hf

where E is the energy, h is Planck's constant (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s), and f is the frequency.

Geometrical Optics

Geometrical optics is a branch of optics that describes the propagation of light as rays. It is used to analyze phenomena like reflection, refraction, and image formation in lenses and mirrors.

Key Concepts:
  • Light as Rays: In geometrical optics, light is treated as traveling in straight lines called light rays.
  • Reflection: The bouncing of light off surfaces.
    θᵢ = θʳ
    where θᵢ is the angle of incidence (the angle between the incident ray and the normal) and θʳ is the angle of reflection (the angle between the reflected ray and the normal).
  • Refraction: The bending of light as it passes from one medium to another with a different refractive index.
    n₁ sin(θ₁) = n₂ sin(θ₂)
    where n₁ and n₂ are the refractive indices of the first and second media, and θ₁ and θ₂ are the angles of incidence and refraction, respectively.
  • Refractive Index: The refractive index n is given by:
    n = c/v
    where c is the speed of light in a vacuum and v is the speed of light in the medium.
  • Total Internal Reflection: Occurs when light moves from a denser medium to a rarer medium, and the angle of incidence exceeds a critical angle θc. The critical angle is given by:
    θc = sin⁻¹(n₂/n₁)
    where n₁ > n₂.
Mirrors and Lenses:
  • Mirror Equation: The relationship between the object distance dₒ, the image distance dᵢ, and the focal length f of a spherical mirror is given by:
    1/f = 1/dₒ + 1/dᵢ
  • Lens Equation: For thin lenses, the lens equation is similar:
    1/f = 1/dₒ + 1/dᵢ
  • Magnification (for Mirrors and Lenses): The magnification M is the ratio of the image height hᵢ to the object height hₒ:
    M = hᵢ / hₒ = dᵢ / dₒ
    - For a concave mirror or converging lens, the image can be real (inverted) or virtual (upright) depending on the position of the object. - For a convex mirror or diverging lens, the image is always virtual, upright, and smaller than the object.
Important Formulas in Geometrical Optics:
  • Thin Lens Formula:
    1/f = 1/dₒ + 1/dᵢ
  • Mirror Formula:
    1/f = 1/dₒ + 1/dᵢ
  • Magnification:
    M = hᵢ / hₒ = dᵢ / dₒ
  • Critical Angle (Total Internal Reflection):
    θc = sin⁻¹(n₂ / n₁)
