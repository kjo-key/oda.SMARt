Structure of the Atom

1. Basic Structure of the Atom

An atom consists of:

  • Nucleus: The dense, central core of the atom, containing protons and neutrons.
  • Electron Cloud: The region around the nucleus where electrons are likely to be found. Electrons are negatively charged particles that move in orbitals around the nucleus.

2. Atomic Number and Mass Number

Atomic Number (Z): The number of protons in an atom's nucleus. It defines the element and is unique to each element.

Mass Number (A): The total number of protons and neutrons in an atom’s nucleus.
Formula: A = Z + N

Example: Carbon-12 has 6 protons and 6 neutrons, so its mass number is 12.

3. Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same atomic number) but with a different number of neutrons, hence different mass numbers.

Example: Carbon has both carbon-12 (6 protons, 6 neutrons) and carbon-14 (6 protons, 8 neutrons).

4. Bohr's Model of the Atom

Bohr proposed that electrons orbit the nucleus in fixed paths or shells. Each shell can hold a specific number of electrons, and electrons occupy the lowest available energy level first.

  • Energy levels (shells) are denoted as K, L, M, N, etc.
  • The maximum number of electrons in each shell is given by 2n², where n is the principal quantum number.

5. Quantum Mechanical Model

The quantum mechanical model of the atom describes the behavior of electrons in terms of probabilities rather than fixed paths. Electrons are organized in orbitals.

6. Key Formulas in Atomic Structure

  • Rydberg Formula:
    1 / λ = RH (1 / n12 - 1 / n22)
    Where:
    • λ = Wavelength of emitted or absorbed radiation
    • RH = Rydberg constant (1.097 x 107 m-1)
    • n1, n2 = Principal quantum numbers of the two energy levels
  • Energy of an Electron (Bohr's Model):
    En = - 13.6 / n2 eV
  • Photoelectric Effect:
    E = hf, where h is Planck’s constant and f is the frequency of the photon.

7. Important Concepts Related to Atomic Structure

  • Atomic Mass: The mass of an atom is nearly equal to its mass number (protons + neutrons).
  • Ionization Energy: The energy required to remove an electron from an atom.
  • Electron Affinity: The energy released when an electron is added to a neutral atom.

8. Key Atomic Models and Their Contributions

  • Dalton's Model: Atoms are indivisible.
  • Thomson's Model: The atom is a sphere of positive charge with embedded electrons.
  • Rutherford's Model: The atom consists of a tiny, dense nucleus surrounded by electrons.
  • Bohr's Model: Electrons occupy fixed orbits with quantized energy levels.
