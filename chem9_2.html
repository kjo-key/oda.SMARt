Measurements and Scientific Methods

What is Measurement?

Measurement is the process of obtaining the size, quantity, or amount of something in a systematic and standardized way. It allows scientists to quantify observations and compare them with other data. Measurements are fundamental to science because they enable precise communication of results, reproducibility of experiments, and verification of hypotheses.

Units of Measurement:

  • SI Units: These are the standard units used worldwide for consistency and ease of comparison.
  • Length: meter (m)
  • Mass: kilogram (kg)
  • Time: second (s)
  • Electric Current: ampere (A)
  • Temperature: kelvin (K)
  • Amount of Substance: mole (mol)
  • Luminous Intensity: candela (cd)

Precision vs. Accuracy

Accuracy refers to how close a measured value is to the true or accepted value.

Precision refers to how close multiple measurements are to each other, regardless of whether they are close to the true value.

Types of Measurements

  • Direct Measurement: Taking a measurement directly from an instrument (e.g., measuring the length of an object using a ruler).
  • Indirect Measurement: Using a formula or derived method to calculate a quantity based on others (e.g., calculating the density of a substance using mass and volume).

Significant Figures

Significant figures are the digits in a measurement that carry meaningful information about its precision.

  • All non-zero digits are significant.
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant.
  • Leading zeros are not significant.
  • Trailing zeros are significant only if there is a decimal point.

Errors in Measurement

  • Systematic Error: An error that consistently occurs in the same direction.
  • Random Error: Errors that occur unpredictably and vary from one measurement to another.
  • Human Error: Mistakes made by the observer during measurement.
