Temperature and Thermometry

Temperature

Temperature is a physical quantity that expresses the amount of thermal energy contained in a substance. It determines the direction of heat flow, where heat always flows from a body of higher temperature to one of lower temperature. Temperature is a fundamental concept in thermodynamics, and it is related to the microscopic motion of particles.

Thermodynamic Temperature Scales

Temperature is measured using different scales. The three most commonly used temperature scales are:

  • Celsius (°C): Based on the freezing and boiling points of water at standard atmospheric pressure. The freezing point is 0°C, and the boiling point is 100°C.

    Celsius scale: TC = T - 273.15

  • Fahrenheit (°F): Mainly used in the United States. The freezing point is 32°F, and the boiling point is 212°F.

    Fahrenheit scale: TF = (9/5) × TC + 32

  • Kelvin (K): The SI unit for temperature. It starts at absolute zero (0 K), which is the lowest possible temperature.

    Kelvin scale: TK = TC + 273.15

Thermometry

Thermometry is the science of measuring temperature. It involves various devices and instruments known as thermometers. There are different types of thermometers, including:

  • Mercury Thermometer: Uses mercury, which expands and contracts as the temperature changes.
  • Alcohol Thermometer: Similar to the mercury thermometer but uses alcohol instead of mercury.
  • Digital Thermometer: Uses electronic sensors to measure temperature and display it on a digital screen.
  • Thermocouple: A temperature sensor consisting of two different conductors that produce a voltage related to the temperature difference between them.
  • Resistance Temperature Detector (RTD): Uses the fact that the resistance of a material changes with temperature to measure temperature.

Important Concepts in Temperature and Thermometry

  • Thermal Expansion: As temperature increases, most materials expand. The change in length, area, or volume of a material due to a change in temperature can be expressed mathematically.
    • Linear Expansion: For solids, the change in length is given by:

      ΔL = α L0 ΔT

    • Area Expansion: The change in area is:

      ΔA = 2α A0 ΔT

    • Volume Expansion: The change in volume is:

      ΔV = β V0 ΔT

  • Thermal Conductivity: Describes the ability of a material to conduct heat. It is defined by Fourier's Law:

    Q = -kA ΔT / d

  • Ideal Gas Law (for Temperature and Pressure Relationship): The ideal gas law relates pressure, volume, and temperature of an ideal gas:

    PV = nRT

  • Heat Capacity and Specific Heat: The heat capacity of a body is the amount of heat required to change its temperature by one degree.
    • Formula for heat capacity:

      Q = mcΔT

    • Formula for specific heat:

      c = Q / (m ΔT)

  • Latent Heat: When a substance changes its phase (from solid to liquid, or liquid to gas), heat is required without a change in temperature. This is known as latent heat.
    • Latent heat of fusion (for melting):

      Q = mLf

    • Latent heat of vaporization (for boiling):

      Q = mLv

Summary of Key Formulas

  • Celsius to Kelvin: TK = TC + 273.15
  • Celsius to Fahrenheit: TF = (9/5) × TC + 32
  • Fahrenheit to Celsius: TC = (5/9)(TF - 32)
  • Linear Expansion: ΔL = α L0 ΔT
  • Area Expansion: ΔA = 2α A0 ΔT
  • Volume Expansion: ΔV = β V0 ΔT
  • Thermal Conductivity: Q = -kA ΔT / d
  • Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT
  • Heat Capacity: Q = mcΔT
  • Latent Heat: Q = mL
