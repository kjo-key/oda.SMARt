Physics and Physical Quantities

Physical Quantities: An Overview

Physical quantities are the properties of matter and energy that can be measured. They can be classified into two broad categories: Scalar quantities and Vector quantities.

1. Scalar Quantities

Scalar quantities are those that are fully described by a magnitude (numerical value) alone. They do not have a direction associated with them.

  • Mass (m): The amount of matter in an object, measured in kilograms (kg).
  • Time (t): A measure of the duration of events, measured in seconds (s).
  • Temperature (T): A measure of the heat content of a body, measured in Kelvin (K) or Celsius (°C).
  • Energy (E): The capacity to do work, measured in joules (J).
  • Speed (v): The rate of motion, measured in meters per second (m/s).

Formula Example for Speed:

        v = d / t
        Where:
        v = speed (m/s),
        d = distance (m),
        t = time (s).

2. Vector Quantities

Vector quantities are those that require both a magnitude and a direction to be fully described.

  • Displacement (s): A vector that describes the change in position of an object, measured in meters (m).
  • Velocity (v): The rate of change of displacement, measured in meters per second (m/s).
  • Force (F): An interaction that causes an object to change its motion, measured in newtons (N).
  • Acceleration (a): The rate of change of velocity, measured in meters per second squared (m/s²).
  • Momentum (p): The product of an object's mass and velocity, measured in kilogram meters per second (kg·m/s).

Formula Example for Velocity:

        v = Δs / Δt
        Where:
        v = velocity (m/s),
        Δs = change in position (m),
        Δt = change in time (s).

Fundamental Physical Quantities

1. Length (L)

Length is a fundamental physical quantity representing the distance between two points. It is typically measured in meters (m).

        L = |x2 - x1|

2. Mass (m)

Mass is the measure of the amount of matter in an object, typically measured in kilograms (kg). Mass is a scalar quantity and remains constant regardless of location.

3. Time (t)

Time is the duration of events and is measured in seconds (s). It is a scalar quantity and is a basic dimension in physics.

Derived Physical Quantities

1. Force (F)

Force is an interaction that changes the motion of an object. The unit of force is the newton (N), where 1 N = 1 kg·m/s².

        F = m * a
        Where:
        F = force (N),
        m = mass (kg),
        a = acceleration (m/s²).

2. Work (W)

Work is the transfer of energy when a force is applied to an object causing displacement. The unit of work is the joule (J).

        W = F * d * cos(θ)
        Where:
        W = work (J),
        F = force (N),
        d = displacement (m),
        θ = angle between the force and displacement direction.

3. Energy (E)

Energy is the capacity to do work. It is measured in joules (J).

  • Kinetic Energy (KE): The energy of motion. 
    KE = ½ * m * v²
  • Potential Energy (PE): The energy stored due to an object’s position or configuration. 
    PE = m * g * h

4. Power (P)

Power is the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred. The unit of power is the watt (W).

        P = W / t
        Where:
        P = power (W),
        W = work (J),
        t = time (s).

5. Momentum (p)

Momentum is the product of an object’s mass and velocity. The unit of momentum is kilogram meter per second (kg·m/s).

        p = m * v
        Where:
        p = momentum (kg·m/s),
        m = mass (kg),
        v = velocity (m/s).

6. Acceleration (a)

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. The unit of acceleration is meters per second squared (m/s²).

        a = Δv / Δt
        Where:
        a = acceleration (m/s²),
        Δv = change in velocity (m/s),
        Δt = change in time (s).

7. Pressure (P)

Pressure is the force applied per unit area. The unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa) 

        P = F / A
        Where:
        P = pressure (Pa),
        F = force (N),
        A = area (m²).

SI Units and Dimensional Formula

The SI system (International System of Units) is used globally for measuring physical quantities. Each physical quantity has a corresponding unit and a dimensional formula that shows how the fundamental quantities (mass, length, and time) combine to form the unit.

Examples:

  • Force (F): SI unit = newton (N), dimensional formula = [M L T-2].
  • Energy (E): SI unit = joule (J), dimensional formula = [M L2 T-2].
  • Power (P): SI unit = watt (W), dimensional formula = [M L2 T-3].
