Static and Current Electricity

Static Electricity

Static electricity refers to the buildup of electric charge on the surface of objects. It occurs when electrons are transferred between materials, typically through friction. The charges accumulate and remain on the object until they are discharged.

Key Concepts:

  • Charge: A property of matter that causes it to experience a force when placed in an electric and magnetic field. There are two types of charge: positive and negative.
    • Positive Charge: Results from a deficit of electrons.
    • Negative Charge: Results from an excess of electrons.
  • Coulomb’s Law: Describes the force between two point charges.

F = ke q1 q2 / r2

Where:

  • F = force between two charges (in Newtons)
  • q1, q2 = magnitudes of the two charges (in Coulombs)
  • r = distance between the charges (in meters)
  • ke = Coulomb’s constant (8.99 × 109 N⋅m2 / C2)

Electric Fields:

An electric field is a field around a charged particle that exerts a force on other charged particles.

E = F / q

Where:

  • E = electric field (in Newtons per Coulomb, N/C)
  • F = force experienced by a test charge (in Newtons)
  • q = charge (in Coulombs)

Electric Potential:

The electric potential is the work needed to move a charge from infinity to a point in space. It is related to potential energy per unit charge.

V = U / q

Where:

  • V = electric potential (in Volts)
  • U = electric potential energy (in Joules)
  • q = charge (in Coulombs)

Current Electricity

Current electricity deals with the flow of electric charge, typically through conductors like wires. Electric current is the rate of flow of charge.

Key Concepts:

  • Electric Current (I): The rate of flow of charge.

I = Q / t

Where:

  • I = current (in Amperes, A)
  • Q = charge (in Coulombs)
  • t = time (in seconds)
  • Voltage (V): The potential difference between two points in a circuit. It drives the current to flow.

V = I R

Where:

  • V = voltage (in Volts, V)
  • I = current (in Amperes)
  • R = resistance (in Ohms, Ω)
  • Ohm’s Law: Describes the relationship between current, voltage, and resistance.

V = I R

Resistance:

Resistance (R) is the opposition to the flow of current in a conductor. It depends on the material, length, and cross-sectional area of the conductor.

R = ρ L / A

Where:

  • R = resistance (in Ohms, Ω)
  • ρ = resistivity (in Ohm-meters)
  • L = length of the conductor (in meters)
  • A = cross-sectional area (in square meters)

Power in Electric Circuits:

Electric Power (P) is the rate at which electrical energy is consumed or produced.

P = V I = I2 R = V2 / R

Where:

  • P = power (in Watts, W)
  • V = voltage (in Volts)
  • I = current (in Amperes)
  • R = resistance (in Ohms)

Kirchhoff’s Laws:

  • Kirchhoff’s Current Law (KCL): The total current entering a junction equals the total current leaving the junction.

∑ Iin = ∑ Iout

  • Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law (KVL): The sum of all electrical potential differences (voltages) around any closed loop or circuit is zero.

∑ V = 0

Key Formulas Summary:

  • Coulomb’s Law: F = ke q1 q2 / r2
  • Electric Field: E = F / q
  • Electric Potential: V = U / q
  • Current: I = Q / t
  • Ohm’s Law: V = I R
  • Power: P = V I, P = I2 R, P = V2 / R
  • Resistance: R = ρ L / A

Summary of Static vs. Current Electricity:

  • Static electricity involves the accumulation of charges on surfaces and the resulting forces between them.
  • Current electricity deals with the flow of charges through conductors and the associated electrical circuits.
