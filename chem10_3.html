Inorganic Compounds

A deep dive into the world of inorganic compounds

1. Classification of Inorganic Compounds

a. Acids

Acids are compounds that can donate protons (H⁺ ions) or accept electron pairs. They have a sour taste, turn litmus paper red, and can react with bases to form salts and water (neutralization). Acids are typically made by dissolving non-metals in water.

  • Example: Hydrochloric acid (HCl), Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄)
    Key formula:
    - Monobasic Acid: HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻
    - Diprotic Acid: H₂SO₄ → 2H⁺ + SO₄²⁻

b. Bases

Bases are compounds that can accept protons (H⁺ ions) or donate electron pairs. They are often bitter in taste and slippery to the touch. Bases can neutralize acids to form salts and water.

  • Example: Sodium hydroxide (NaOH), Ammonia (NH₃)
    Key formula:
    - Sodium hydroxide: NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻
    - Ammonia in water: NH₃ + H₂O → NH₄⁺ + OH⁻

c. Salts

Salts are formed when an acid reacts with a base. They are made up of the positive ion (cation) from the base and the negative ion (anion) from the acid. Salts are typically crystalline and soluble in water.

  • Example: Sodium chloride (NaCl), Calcium carbonate (CaCO₃)
    Key formula:
    - Sodium chloride: Na⁺ + Cl⁻
    - Calcium carbonate: Ca²⁺ + CO₃²⁻

d. Oxides

Oxides are compounds that contain at least one oxygen atom and one other element. They are generally classified into two groups:

  • Basic Oxides: Oxides of metals that react with water to form bases.
  • Acidic Oxides: Oxides of non-metals that react with water to form acids.
  • Example:
    • Basic Oxide: Sodium oxide (Na₂O) → Na₂O + H₂O → 2NaOH
    • Acidic Oxide: Carbon dioxide (CO₂) → CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃

e. Hydrates

Hydrates are inorganic compounds that include water molecules in their crystal structure. The water is usually chemically bonded to the compound and can be released by heating.

  • Example: Copper(II) sulfate pentahydrate (CuSO₄·5H₂O)

f. Coordination Compounds

Coordination compounds consist of a central metal atom or ion bonded to surrounding molecules or ions called ligands. These compounds are commonly found in transition metals and play important roles in biological systems, like hemoglobin in blood.

  • Example: [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻ (Hexacyanoferrate(II))

g. Peroxides

Peroxides are compounds that contain an oxygen-oxygen single bond. They are used in various applications, including as bleaching agents and disinfectants.

  • Example: Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂)
    Key formula:
    - Hydrogen peroxide: H₂O₂ → H₂O + O₂ (decomposes into water and oxygen)

2. Inorganic Compound Formulas

The formulas of inorganic compounds are derived based on their constituent ions or elements. Here are a few examples with their corresponding formulas:

  • Sodium chloride (NaCl)
  • Calcium carbonate (CaCO₃)

  • Potassium permanganate (KMnO₄)
  • Copper(II) sulfate (CuSO₄)
  • Magnesium oxide (MgO)
  • Iron(III) oxide (Fe₂O₃)

3. Key Concepts in Inorganic Chemistry

a. Ionic Bonding

Inorganic compounds frequently form ionic bonds, where one atom (usually a metal) donates electrons to another atom (usually a non-metal), resulting in positively charged ions (cations) and negatively charged ions (anions).

b. Covalent Bonding

In some inorganic compounds, atoms share electrons to form covalent bonds. This is common in compounds like water (H₂O) and carbon dioxide (CO₂).

c. Acid-Base Reactions

Acids and bases react in a process known as neutralization. When an acid reacts with a base, it produces water and a salt.

    Example:
    HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

d. Oxidation-Reduction Reactions (Redox)

In redox reactions, one substance loses electrons (oxidation) and another gains electrons (reduction). Inorganic compounds like peroxides and transition metal complexes frequently participate in redox reactions.

4. Important Inorganic Compounds and Their Uses

  • Water (H₂O): The most common inorganic compound, crucial for life and many chemical processes.
  • Ammonium nitrate (NH₄NO₃): Used in fertilizers and explosives.
  • Sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO₃): Baking soda, used in cooking, cleaning, and as an antacid.
  • Calcium chloride (CaCl₂): Used for de-icing roads and in drying agents.
  • Magnesium sulfate (MgSO₄): Used in Epsom salts, in medicine, and in agriculture.
